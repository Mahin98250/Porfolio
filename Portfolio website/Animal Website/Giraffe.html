The Giraffe

The giraffe is an African artiodactyl mammal, the tallest living terrestrial animal, and the largest ruminant on Earth. Its generic name translates to "fast-walking camel-leopard".

Physical Characteristics

  • Height: The most notable feature is their immense height. Adult males can reach 5.7 meters (18.7 feet) tall, with their neck alone often exceeding 2 meters (6 feet) in length.
  • Neck and Vertebrae: Despite its extraordinary length, the giraffe's neck contains only seven cervical vertebrae—the same number as humans—though each vertebra can be over 28 cm (11 inches) long.
  • Coat and Patterns: Each giraffe possesses a unique coat pattern, much like human fingerprints. These patches are a complex network of brown, cream, orange, and black, which serve as effective camouflage.
  • Ossicones: Both males and females have prominent, hair-covered, horn-like structures on top of their heads called ossicones, which are made of ossified cartilage.

Habitat and Behavior

Giraffes inhabit savannas and open woodlands across Africa. They are herbivores that primarily feed on leaves, flowers, and fruits, especially from the thorny acacia tree.

  • Diet & Digestion: They are ruminants and spend many hours per day feeding, consuming up to 34 kg (75 lbs) of foliage daily. Their long, tough tongues allow them to navigate thorny branches easily.
  • Social Structure: Giraffes are highly social animals that live in fission-fusion societies, meaning herd size and composition change frequently. They often form bonds and move across vast home ranges.
  • Sleep: Due to potential predator threats (mainly lions), giraffes have one of the shortest sleep requirements of any mammal, often sleeping in brief bursts totaling as little as 30 minutes a day while standing up.

Conservation Status

Once classified as a single species with several subspecies, recent genetic studies suggest there may be multiple distinct species of giraffe. The overall population status is listed as **Vulnerable** on the [IUCN Red List](www.iucnredlist.org) due to habitat loss, fragmentation, and poaching pressures across their African range.
