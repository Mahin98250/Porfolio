The Zebra


Zebras are several species of African equids (horse family) known for their distinctive black-and-white striped coats. There are three main species: the Plains Zebra (most common), the Grévy's Zebra (largest and most endangered), and the Mountain Zebra.

Physical Characteristics

  • The Stripes: Every zebra has a unique pattern of stripes, like a fingerprint. These patterns serve several theories for survival, including camouflage (disrupting their outline from predators), a visual defense against biting flies (confusing the insects' vision), and potentially social signaling or thermoregulation (cooling the body).
  • Coloration: Though their stripes are iconic, debates about whether they are black with white stripes or white with black stripes persist. Scientifically, zebras have black skin underneath their fur.
  • Size: Plains zebras typically stand around 1.2 to 1.5 meters (47 to 59 inches) at the shoulder and weigh up to 450 kg (992 lbs). Grévy's zebras are significantly larger.
  • Mane and Tail: Unlike horses, zebras have stiff, erect manes that stand straight up, and long, flowing tails.

Habitat and Behavior

Zebras inhabit a wide variety of habitats, including savannas, grasslands, woodlands, and mountainous regions, depending on the species.

  • Highly Social: Zebras are highly social animals that live in herds. Plains and Mountain zebras form smaller, stable family groups (harems) consisting of one stallion, several mares, and their foals. Grévy's zebras have a more loosely structured, territorial social system.
  • Communication: They communicate using a variety of vocalizations, including braying, snorting, and barking.
  • Migration: Plains zebras are famous for undertaking long annual migrations in search of fresh grazing grounds and water, often migrating alongside wildebeest across the Serengeti ecosystem.

Conservation Status

The conservation status varies by species. The Plains Zebra is currently listed as **Near Threatened**, while both the Grévy's Zebra and the Mountain Zebra are listed as **Endangered** on the [IUCN Red List](www.iucnredlist.org). Major threats include habitat degradation, competition with livestock for resources, and illegal hunting.
